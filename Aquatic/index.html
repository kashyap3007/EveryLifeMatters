Aquatic Life

Aquatic life means fish, invertebrates, amphibians and other flora and fauna which inhabit waters of the state at some stage of their life cycles. Aquatic life does not include human pathogens or insect pests, aquatic invasive species or other organisms which may be considered “undesirable” by the Wyoming Game and Fish Department or U.S. Fish and Wildlife Service within their appropriate jurisdictions. Different Type of Fishes

The total number of living fish species—about 32,000— is greater than the total of all other vertebrate species (amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals) combined.

Lampreys

Lampreys are jawless fish that use a multi-toothed, sucker-like mouth to attach to other animals as a parasite. The Sea Lamprey (Petromyzon marinus) is native to the North Atlantic, where it does not kill its host animal — and is invasive to the Great Lakes, and rivers.

Catfish

Catfish are a collection of species that are members of about 35 different fish families. They are distinguished by their barbels, which are whisker-like feelers that are usually situated on the top part of their mouths (the upper mandible). Catfish live both in fresh and salt waters.

more info

Tuna

Tuna are large, saltwater fish species (consisting of the Katsuwonus, Auxis, and Thunnus genera, among others) that can swim up to 43 miles per hour and weigh up to 2,000 pounds. Noted as a highly popular commercial fish, the tuna can swim great distances to feed and spawn.

Latest info

Herring is a saltwater fish that belong to the genus Clupeus. They are found in the colder waters of the North Atlantic and are known to be scaly fish with forked tails that swim largely in schools. Herring are a popular culinary fish that can be pickled, salted, and smoked.

Mudskippers are one of the few fish species that can travel on land. Native to the swamps of Myanmar, Thailand, India, Indonesia, and the Philippines, mudskippers have chambers where they can store oxygen, which are located behind closable gill flaps.

Fish care guide

01

The bigger the tank or aquarium the better

Most fish may be small in size, but they still need plenty of room to swim, especially if you have decided to get more than one. Whether you’re starting with a single goldfish or a few, browse our wide range of fish tanks for their new home.

02

Add an air pump.

This will keep the water in the tank oxygenated and keep it moving which is great for your fish. Pumps come in different sizes depending on the amount of litres your tank can hold. Our range of air pumps can provide a healthier underwater environment for your fish.

03

Add some gravel to the tank

Some bacteria is beneficial to fish and the gravel gives it somewhere to live. It will also help to break down any waste that your fish creates, as well as adding an attractive look to their surroundings.
